Authors: Wen Helena Li; Francesco Chirico; Robert E. Hoskisson
Affiliations: University of Technology Sydney, NSW, Australia; Macquarie University, Sydney, NSW, Australia; Jönköping University, Sweden; Rice University, Houston, TX, USA
The evidence is mixed regarding family firms focus on green innovation. Using the socioemotional wealth perspective, we argue that family control have two competing logics, stewardship and conservatism, leading to divergent green innovation outcomes. Integrating an institutional contingency perspective, we show that local government environmental attention shapes which logic prevails. Using listed Chinese family firm data, we find that high (low) local environmental attention strengthens (weakens) the relationship between family control and green innovation, particularly when firms are geographically closer to local governments. Interestingly, highly controlled family firms are least environmentally responsive when close to governments with low environmental attention.

Abstract This paper has three aims. First, it defines the Matthew effect in societies with multiple groups of entities and explores its connection to the butterfly effect from chaos theory, uncovering shared mechanisms that drive inequality—a novel contribution. Second, it presents a method to detect the Matthew effect in both theoretical models and empirical data. Third, it argues that multilayer social network modeling provides the most suitable framework for explaining the Matthew effect when observed in data. By addressing its definition, measurement, and theoretical explanation, this paper offers a comprehensive framework for analyzing the Matthew effect and its role in shaping societal dynamics.
