Agricultural trade and adaptation to climate change in sub-Saharan Africa
2026-07-14 | DOI: 10.1093/ej/ueag094 | The Economic Journal
Authors: Obie Porteous
Affiliations: Middlebury College ,
Abstract Climate change is predicted to have large and heterogeneous impacts on agricultural yields. I use a model of production, consumption, and trade of 24 crops between 271 regions across sub-Saharan Africa to simulate the effects of climate change and explore how trade mediates these effects. I estimate a continent-wide welfare loss equivalent to 2.4% of GDP using median yield changes across 5 climate models, with country-level losses up to 60% of GDP in the worst model. The continent-wide welfare loss is 19% smaller with lower trade costs and over 7 times larger if trade flows are capped at current levels.

Abstract This paper analyzes the political processes by which time zones are locally managed and changed, and how these are converted into global standards in the absence of an international time zone standardization body. We argue that the persistence of a singular global time zone standard depends on technical infrastructures operating largely independently of state and non-state actors. The apparent neutrality of time zones as technical artifacts supporting international coordination required for economic globalization obscures locally negotiated practices shaped by social, biological, political, and economic rationales. The study highlights how globally uniform technical systems can coexist with locally differentiated yet politically contested practices.
