Abstract We identify the distributional and quantile marginal treatment effect functions when the outcome is right-censored. Our method requires a conditionally exogenous instrument and random censoring. The Factor Bias of External Inputs: Implications for Substitution between Capital and Labor

Authors: Dimitrije Ruzic
Affiliations: INSEAD and CEPR
Abstract This paper reevaluates the longstanding debate on capital-labor substitution by examining the role of external inputs: raw materials, intermediate goods and services, imports, offshoring. Both a meta-analysis (analyzing existing estimates of substitution) and direct estimation (using U.S. data for 1963-2016) indicate that external inputs disproportionately displace labor. These findings imply (1) that the capital-labor ratio responds 40-80% more strongly to the price of labor than to the price of capital, (2) that value added cannot be modeled separably from gross output, and (3) that historical disagreements regarding substitution can be recast as an omitted variable bias involving external inputs.

Abstract We study the effect of the MeToo movement on reports of sex crimes to the police. Using multiple empirical strategies, including a triple-difference design comparing crime types across countries and over time, we find that the movement increased reported sex crimes by approximately 10%. Analyzing incident-level US data, we find that the movement also increased arrests and had similar effects across racial and socioeconomic groups. Abstract We study how public disclosure of politicians’ outside income affects their behavior.
